Why Take the Start Assessment Test?

Taking the start assessment test can help you determine if you have any symptoms or risk factors associated with thyroid disorders. Early detection and treatment of thyroid issues can prevent complications and improve your overall health and well-being.

By identifying any potential thyroid problems early on, you can take proactive steps to manage your thyroid health and prevent serious complications.

It's recommended to take the start assessment test regularly, especially if you have a family history of thyroid disorders or if you experience symptoms such as fatigue, weight changes, or mood swings.

  • Thyroid

    Thyroid is a small, butterfly-shaped gland located at the front of your neck under your skin. Thyroid is an important endocrine gland that makes and releases certain hormones.

  • Hypothyroid

    Hypothyroidism happens when the thyroid gland doesn't make enough thyroid hormone. This condition also is called underactive thyroid.


  • Hyperthyroid

    Hyperthyroidism happens when the thyroid gland makes too much thyroid hormone. This condition also is called overactive thyroid.

Self Check

How to check Thyroid at home?

Start by identifying where your thyroid is located. Generally, you’ll find the thyroid on the front of your neck, between your collar bone and Adam’s apple. In men, the Adam’s apple is much easier to see. For women, it’s usually easiest to look from the collar bone up. Tip your head back while looking in a mirror. Look at your neck and try to hone in on the space you will be looking once you start the exam. Once you’re ready, take a drink of water while your head is tilted back. Watch your thyroid as you swallow. During this test, you’re looking for lumps or bumps. You may be able to see them when you swallow the water. Repeat this test a few times to get a good look at your thyroid. If you see any lumps or bumps, reach out to your healthcare provider.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is a normal thyroid test range?

    A normal thyroid test range varies slightly based on the lab, but it typically falls within the following guidelines: TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone): 0.3-4.5 mIU/L (10.3-362.9 pmol/L) Free T4 (thyroxine): 80-160 pmol/L Free T3 (triiodothyronine): 20-60 pmol/L



  • What are the benefits of a TSH test?

    A TSH test provides valuable information about your thyroid function. An elevated TSH level may indicate an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism), while a decreased TSH level may indicate an overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism). Additionally, a normal TSH level is essential for proper thyroid hormone production.

  • Can thyroid disorders be prevented?

    While not always preventable, maintaining a balanced diet, managing stress, and avoiding excessive iodine intake may contribute to thyroid health.
